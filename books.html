📚 Quest Packs & Adventures

Here's the deal: I write about serious stuff; philosophy, society, why we are here and why bananas are curved,
but I wrap it in stories that involve a sarcastic hoopoe, a snack‑obsessed student named Jimba, his classmates,
and a classroom that accidentally travels through time and space.

Fair warning: Some of these dives go very deep. Like, past the bottom of the rabbit hole deep.
You might finish one and start questioning your existence, your grocery list, and whether Die Hard is a Christmas movie (it is).
That's normal. Or not. I'm not a therapist.

"Climate Change Denial" – a short story. Download available soon

"It's just weather," Uncle Tarek said, waving at the flooded schoolyard. "The climate has always changed." Amina wanted to argue. She had the graphs, the articles, the police records. But her uncle had never been wrong in front of her. She bit her lip. "Let's walk," she said. Maybe the drowned tomato plants could speak louder than she could.

"Wealth inequality & Zakat" – a short story. Download available soon

Layla watched Aya take a second bread roll and slip it into her pocket. She knew the food bank was for families who needed help. But Aya never said anything. Never asked. Now Layla's father was bragging about their new car. She wondered: was giving zakat only about money? Or about seeing what you didn't want to see?

"Stereotypes" – a short story. Download available soon

Tariq could jump higher than anyone. But the volleyball team was "for girls." The boys laughed when he asked to try out. "You'll get hurt," they said. "It's not football." He didn't answer. He just walked to the court, picked up the ball, and served. The net shook. The gym went quiet. He still didn't know if he would make the team, but he knew he had to try.

"AI & Honesty" – a short story. Download available soon

The essay won first place. Everyone clapped. But Nour felt the letters burning in his pocket, the ones he typed, not the ones he wrote. The AI had done the work. He had only pressed "generate." Now Mr. Zakaria wanted him to read it aloud at assembly. Nour looked at the podium. He had a choice: speak another's words, or speak his own silence.

"Animal Testing" – a short story. Download available soon

Aisha hid the rabbit in the art supply closet before class. She knew she couldn't keep it secret forever. Ms. Magda wanted data, skin reaction, redness, swelling. "It's one small animal," the teacher said. "For science. For human good." But Aisha looked at the rabbit's nose, soft and twitching. She thought of her grandmother's words: No creature is small enough to be forgotten.

"Cultural Appropriation" – a short story. Download available soon

Leila watched Fatima put on the dirac – the Somali dress her own grandmother had sewn. Fatima called it "exotic." She would wear it for a fashion show, then return to her own clothes. Leila's grandmother stood at the back, silent. Afterward, Leila took Fatima aside. "Would you wear my skin, too?" she asked. Fatima blinked. The question hung like a dress on a hook.

"Freedom of Speech VS Respect for Beliefs" – a short story. Download available soon

"It's just a joke," Sami said, holding up the drawing. A few classmates laughed. More didn't. Bilal stood up. "Would you draw my mother like that?" he asked. Sami's smile faded. He hadn't thought of it that way. He had only thought about his freedom. Now Mr. Ameen was writing on the board: Your freedom ends where someone else's dignity begins. Sami erased the drawing.

"Water Wars (Geopolitics)" – a short story. Download available soon

"Your dam is stealing our water," Youssef said. "Your farms use too much," Alem replied. They stood on opposite sides of the model river they were supposed to build together. Ms. Molly didn't intervene. She just placed two empty cups on the table. "Share," she said. "One cup. Two sides. Figure it out." Youssef looked at the cup. Alem looked at Youssef.
